    The frame story
  • 1.2–8 janamejayavaiśampāyanasaṃvādaḥ: Janamejaya remains unsatisfied after hearing the Mahābhārata and asks Vaiśampāyana about the Secret Dharma. In reply, Vaiśampāyana starts relating a dialogue between Vigatarāga (= Viṣṇu in disguise), and Anarthayajña, an ascetic, Vigatarāga testing Anarthayajña with the following questions

    [`VAIṢṆAVA LAYER' STARTS]

    Vigatarāga and Anarthayajña
  • 1.9–10 Vigatarāga's question on brahmavidyā
  • 1.11–31 kālapāśaḥ: his questions on the human body, on time and death, which leads to an enumeration of the divisions of time from kalā to years, from yugas to kalpas
  • 1.32–36 parārdhādi: numbers, the powers of ten from one to two hundred quadrillion (para)
    Brahmā's Egg
  • 1.37–59 brahmāṇḍam: Brahmā's Egg, the names of cosmic rulers
  • 1.60–63 pramāṇam: distances in the universe
  • 1.64–77 the descent of the Purāṇas; names of their redactors from Brahmā to Dvaipāyana and Romaharṣa
Chapter 2 (open)

    Śiva's World
  • 2.1–3 Vigatarāga's question: the term śivāṇḍa has been mentioned, what is that?
  • 2.4–40 Anarthayajña replies: Śiva's world is a sort of heaven without desires, anger, disease, yugas, etc.; its dimensions are given; its divisions belong to Īśāna, Tatpuruṣa, Aghora, Sadyojāta and Vāmadeva; it is approachable by śivayoga and not by rituals; the last stanza mentions that kāla destroys even Dharma
Chapter 3 (open)

    On Dharma
  • 3.1–2 Vigatarāga's further questions: What is Dharma? How many embodiments does he have? Why is he known as a bull? How many legs does he have? How many are his paths? Whose son is Dharma? How many children does he have?
  • 3.3–7 Anarthayajña explains the etimology of dharma; that it has four legs, the four āśramas; there are five paths of Dharma; Dharma's father is Brahmā; Dharma has thirteen wives
  • 3.8–13 Dharma's wives and children enumerated
  • 3.14–15 Dharma's two embodiments are Śruti and Smṛti

    [Section on the Yama-Niyama rules starts]

    Yama-rules
  • yama 1 = ahiṃsā: Non-violence
    • 3.16–23 five types of violence to avoid:
      1. frightening people
      2. beating people
      3. tying someone up
      4. killing someone
      5. destroying people’s livelihood
    • 3.24–32 praise of Non-violence
    • 3.33–42 one should refrain from killing animals and eating meat; praise of Non-violence; some animals live happily without eating meat; monkeys defeated Rākṣasas without eating meat
Chapter 4 (open)
  • yama 2 = satya: Truthfulness
    • 4.1 definition of Truth
    • 4.2–5 cases where not telling the truth is right
    • 4.6–17 praise or Truthfulness
  • yama 3 = asteya: Refraining from stealing
    • 4.18–24 five kinds of stealth
      1. theft
      2. bribery
      3. cheating with weights
      4. cheating with scales
      5. robbery
    • 4.25–30 praise of refraining from stealing and condemination of stealing and cheating
  • yama 4 = ānṛśaṃsya: Absence of cruelty
    • 4.31 five kinds of cruelty
      1. towards aṣṭamūrti Śiva
      2. towards one's mother
      3. towards one's father
      4. towards cows
      5. towards guests
    • 4.32 eight-formed Śiva is the five elements, the Sun, the Moon and the sacrificer
    • 4.33–34 the father and the mother
    • 4.35–39 praise of the cow and of the five products of the cow
    • 4.40–49 praise of the guest and of those who respect the guest; mention of the mongoose story of the Mahābhārata
  • yama 5 = dama: Self-restraint
    • 4.50–51 praise of Self-restraint
    • 4.52–55 without Self-restraint, the five sense-faculties lead one to death, as in the case of the elephant (touch), the fish (taste), the moth (seeing), the bee (smelling) and the deer (hearing) [see Aśvaghoṣa's Buddhacarita 11.35]
    • 4.56–58 examples of people perishing because of a lack of self-restraint are given: Purūravas, Daṇḍaka, Sagara’s sons, Rāvaṇa, Saudāsa, the Yādavas, Māndhātṛ, Nahuṣa, Mahābali, Arjuna, King Nala, Nṛga [cf.\ Aśvaghoṣa's Buddhacarita 11.13–17]
    • 4.59 warning against a lack of self-restraint
  • yama 6 = ghṛṇā: Taboos
    • 4.60–67 five taboos
      1. approaching women who are not depending on oneself
      2. taking away others' wealth
      3. taking away others' lives
      4. hurting others
      5. eating others' food
  • yama 7 = pañcadhanyavidhi
    • 4.68 the five methods of virtue
      1. 4.69 four cases of observing silence (caturmauna)
        1. instead of violent words
        2. slanderous words
        3. lies
        4. idle talk
      2. 4.70 the four enemies (catuḥśatru)
        1. desire
        2. anger
        3. greed
        4. delusion
      3. 4.71 the four sanctuaries (caturāyatana = the four Buddhist brahmavihāras)
        1. compassion
        2. sympathy in joy
        3. indifference
        4. benevolence
      4. 4.72–73 the four meditations (caturdhyāna)
        1. of the Self
        2. of vidyā
        3. of bhava [= Śiva?]
        4. of the subtle one (sūkṣma)
      5. 4.74 the four legged Dharma (catuṣpāda)
        1. the householder
        2. the chaste student
        3. the forest-dweller
        4. the mendicant
    • 4.75–76 he who knows these will prosper
  • yama 8 = apramāda: Absence of Negligence
    • 4.77–82 five areas of negligence (the mahāpātakas, quotes from Manu)
      1. murdering a Brahmin
      2. drinking alcohol
      3. stealing
      4. having sex with the guru’s wife
      5. when one is connected with these sins or with people involved in these sinful acts
  • yama 9 = mādhurya: Charm
    • 4.83–85 five types of Charm
      1. bodily
      2. verbal
      3. mental
      4. of the eyes
      5. of one's thoughts
  • yama 10 = ārjava: Sincerity
    • 4.86–88 five types of Sincerity
      1. in action
      2. in livelihood
      3. in prosperity
      4. in gratifying others
      5. ... ?
  • 4.89 Śaiva closing verse of the Yama section in Mālinī
