Chapter Four

The second yama-rule: Truthfulness

Anarthayajña spoke:

|4.1| The real nature of things (sadbhāva) is called Truth. Alternatively, it is the same as the direct perception of proof. Relating the sense that corresponds to reality is called the ‘relating the truth’.

|4.2| He who endures severe abuse and beating but keeps quiet, his self being conquered, is called Truth[ful].

|4.3| If somebody asks him any time with a sword lifted to stike him down, then it is not the truth that is to be spoken. [In this case,] a lie is called Truth.

|4.4| If a man who deserves death is walking on the road being afraid, he should not be replied to even if he asks [you]. That is also called Truth.

|4.5| A lie does not hurt when it is connected with joking, with women, O king[!], at the time of marriage, at the departure of life and when all one’s wealth is about to be taken away. They call these five kinds of lies Truth.

|4.6| Since Truth is the supreme Dharma with respect to gods, humans and animals[?], Truth is the best, the most preferable. Truth is the eternal Dharma.

|4.7| Truth is an unmanifest ocean. Truth yields imperishable pleasures. Truth is the ship that carries you to the other world. Truth is the wide path.

|4.8| Truth is said to be the desired path. Truth is the supreme sacrifice. Truth is a pilgrimage place, a supreme pilgrimage place. Truth is the endless donation.

|4.9| Truth is morality, austerity, knowledge. Truth is purity, self-control and tranquillity. Truth is the ladder upwards. Truth is fame and glory and happiness.

|4.10| [When] a thousand Aśvamedha sacrifices and Truth are measured on a pair of scales, Truth indeed surpasses a thousand Aśvamedha sacrifices.

|4.11| The Sun shines with the help of Truth. The Earth stays in place by Truth. The winds blow because of Truth. Water is cooling through Truth.

|4.12| The oceans are there because of the truthful agreement with Priyavrata [Manu’s son]. Govinda abides with Truth because He [as Vāmana] imprisoned [Mahā]Bali.

|4.13| Fire burns with the help of Truth. The Moon rises by Truth. It is because of Truth that the Vindhya mountain stands in place and that the Vardhamāna mountain does not grow [in spite of the fact that its name means ‘growing’].

|4.14| The [mythical] Lokāloka mountains stand by Truth. Mount Meru stands by Truth. The Vedas abide in Truth. Dharma is rooted in Truth.

|4.15| The cow yields milk as Truth. Ghee in milk is there as Truth. The soul dwells in the body in Truth. The eternal soul is Truth.

|4.16| [Tentative translation:] Truth has been reached only by one, who was determined to accomplish it. Only by Rāma, Raghu’s descendant’s heroism can Truth be well-guarded.

|4.17| This is how the rules of Truth have been taught to you, O virtuous one, to favour the whole world. What else do you wish to hear?

|4.18| I can’t have enough of [this teaching of] your[s] Dharma. After this, teach me further, O great ascetic.

The third yama-rule: Refraining from stealing

Anarthayajña spoke:

|4.19| Now listen to stealing, O great Brahmin, which is taught to be of five kinds. Firstly, taking what has not been given, then bribery, cheating with weights, cheating with the scales, and the fifth kind, robbery.

|4.20| Taking what has not been given is when somebody else’s wealth is taken away through a bold crime, ....

|4.21| O great Brahmin, listen to bribery, which defiles Dharma. It is taken in order to destroy a ... capital ... CHECK

|4.22| Therefore it should be considered [stealing?] [because] it is committed out of greed. The method of cheating with weights threatens families.

|4.23| That person should be considered a thief, because he takes away other people’s wealth, when one takes away somebody else’s belongings by the method of cheating with scales.

|4.24| Other people, swindlers and ... have the characteristics of thieves. [If] someone, by deceit or by force, snatches away the wealth

|4.25| of weak and honest people or children, that stupid thief is [rightly] called a thief. There is no sin equal to stealing. There is no Adharma equal to it.

|4.26| There is no ill-fame comparable to being a thief. There is no bad-conduct comparable to being a thief. There is no ignorance like stealing. There is no bigger rouge than a thief.

|4.27| There is no ignorant man comparable to a thief. There is no lazy person comparable to a thief. There is nobody as detestable as a thief. There is nobody as much of an enemy as a thief. There is no such suffering as stealing. There is no man more disgraced than a thief.

|4.28| Some people take away [other people’s] wealth in disguise, some openly. Other wicked people take money from deposits, and some people steal through fraud. Some gather wealth by forging documents, others steal from stolen money??? Others purchase their wealth?? ....... These are considered the vilest.

|4.29| There is no bigger idiot then a thief, who is a wicked person without Dharma and Artha. As long as he lives, he will be trembling in fear of the king, wailing. He will receive his punishment and he will get into severe and ... difficulties, propelled by [his] karma. After some time, he will die and go to hell, weeping vehemently.

|4.30| Having spent ten million aeons of suffering, they emerge from hell to the state of animal existence. Similarly [or ekam?], roaming about in animal existence for a hundred and one times ten million years, then they reach the status of human existence ... full of poverty and disease. Then abandoning all the karmas that are the cause of suffering, one seeks refuge in Śiva.

The fourth yama-rule: Absence of cruelty

|4.31| The one who is hostile towards the eight-formed Śiva, he who hurts his mother or father, he who is hostile to cows or guests: these are the five types of cruel persons.

|4.32| Śiva is evidently of eight forms, with the five elements, and the Sun, the Moon, and the sacrificer. [He who] disgraces [any of these] is a cruel person.

|4.33| The father is to be considered as the sky, he is the cause of our birth. ....

|4.34| The mother is the most venerable [person] on earth. Who would not praise a mother? By that [praise] sacrifices, donations, austerities and [the study of] the Vedas will be fully completed.

|4.35| Cows are a sacred blessing, they are the gods of the gods. Cows contain in themselves all the gods, that’s exactly why one should not hurt them.

|4.36| Cows are the protectors of the new-born world, there is no doubt about it. [The five products of the cow, the pañcagavya:] ghee, milk, curd, and obtaining [cow’s] urine and dung.

|4.37| People who drink the five products of the cow, the five nectars, the five things clarified with a strainer[??], will obtain the fruit of the horse sacrifice, and then will reach the undecaying heavens.

|4.38| There is no wealth comparable to [having] a cow. They yield milk, they draw [a plough etc.], and they roam under the sky. Feeding on grass and issue nectar. When given to Brahmins, they deliver the family [from saṁsāra/suffering].

|4.39| He who never fails to serve the cow daily, and he who is at the cows’ service, will obtain the merits of all sacrifices, austerities and donation [because] he is one who is kind to it (tam?) [i.e. to the cow].

|4.40| He who looks after a guest, he who respects a guest, he who worships a guest, he who praises a guest,

|4.41| he who does not harm a guest, he who does not commit a fault towards a guest, he who does kind things to a guest, he who serves a guest,

|4.42| he who makes a guest satisfied: his merits are endless. He should offer [the guest] a seat, argha-offering, foot-offering, water for washing his feet,

|4.43| or gifts of food and clothes, or all [of these]. He who worships his son’s, wife’s or his own guest[?]

|4.44| with willingness and with a potent and non-hesitating mind, should not ask [the guests about their] gotra, life, studies, country or birth.

|4.45| He should imagine mentally, with devotion, that it is Dharma himself who has come here. Thousands of Aśvamedha sacrifices and hundreds of Rājasūya sacrifices,

|4.46| a thousand of marks on the forehead[? or lotuses?] or the fruit of [visiting] all the pilgrimage places and [performing] all the austerities: he whose guest is satisfied [and] he who can abandon the sentiment of cruelty,

|4.47| will obtain all the merits of the above, there is no doubt about it. ... ?

|4.48| Therefore one should go up to the arriving guest with respectfully joined palms. By one prastha of coarsely ground grains

|4.49| given to a guest, an extremely great sacrifice was performed, and his[?] body reached heaven [CHECK details of the story]. The mongoose related [this story in the Mahābhārata] in the past extensively, O great Brahmin. and you knew it previously. The story of the prastha is well-known.

The fifth yama-rule: Self-restraint

|4.50|

Self-restraint is in itself the collected essence of human Dharma. Self-restraint is Dharma, Self-restraint is heaven, Self-restraint is fame, Self-restraint is happiness.

|4.51| Self-restraint is sacrifice, Self-restraint is a pilgrimage-place, Self-restraint is merit, Self-restraint is religious austerity. Not having Self-restraint is Adharmic, [while] Self-restraint yields a multitude of desired objects.

|4.52| The elephant, the fish, the moth, the bee and the deer are without Self-restraint. The senses are the skin, the tongue, the nose, the eye and the ear.

|4.53| Each of these sense faculties, all are fatal if not conquered. If one conquers Self-restraint, the lack of Self-restraint will die.????

|4.54| In the case of the deer, death comes about because of hearing [HOW?]. Moths die because[?] of their eyes. Bees perish because of smells, fish because of their tongues.

|4.55| The elephant perishes because of touch, not being able to tolerate remaining in fetters. How much more true it is for those who enjoy all five [senses]! Why should it be a wonder that they die?

|4.56| Purūravas perished by excessive greed, Daṇḍaka by excessive desire, Sagara by excessive pride, Rāvaṇa by excessive haughtiness,

|4.57| Saudāsa by excessive anger, the Yādavas by excessive drinking, Māndhātr̥ by excessive desire, Nahuṣa by contempt for Brahmins,

|4.58| Bali perished by excessive donations, Arjuna by excessive heroism, King Nala by excessive gambling, Nr̥ga by taking a cow.

|4.59| [For] a person who is without Self-restraint, O great Brahmin, there is no heaven, liberation or happiness. Brahmins[?] without Self-restraint are the destruction of knowledge, Dharma, family and fame.

The sixth yama-rule: Taboos

|4.60| No person without taboos exists either in this or the other world. In a person without taboos there is no Dharma or religious austerity.

|4.61| These five are taboos: others’ wives, others’ wealth, taking away others’ livelihood, defaming others and others’ food.

|4.62| Listen, O great Brahmin, the wise should always treat others’ wives as a taboo, [be it] a queen, a Brahmin’s wife, a wandering religious mendicant, [and be it] a relative or a non-relative.

|4.63| Listen further to something else, O great Brahmin, with regards to others’ wealth: gaining wealth through unlawful means is when somebody takes away others’ wealth by cheating with [small] weights of an āḍha[ka] or a prastha and with scales.

|4.64| O Brahmin, the wise regards the taking away [others’] livelihood as taboo. ?? ???

|4.65| And what is the defaming of others? Listen, O Brahmin, I’ll tell you briefly. [It refers to] gods, Brahmins, gurus, mothers, guests and enemies.

|4.66| Others’ food and eating together with people whose food should not be eaten (abhojyeṣu) is taboo, [e.g.] after birth or death [in a family], in case of a family having lost their caste, and in the case of a Naṭa [dancer caste?].

|4.67| Those people who cling to the five kinds of taboo [and thus] seek heaven, wealth and liberation, will reach eternal faultlessness in this world, embellished with fame and glory. [A person like that] will obtain wisdom, intelligence, [knowledge of] the Śruti and Smr̥ti traditions, and honour forever. He will be kindness itself[?] and he will obtain an extra long life, no doubt.

The seventh yama-rule: The five methods of virtue?

|4.68| The four ways of observing silence, [victory over] the four enemies, the four supports, the four meditations, and the four legs are called the five ways of being virtuous[?].

|4.69| I shall tell you about the four ways of observing silence. Listen, be attentive. One should avoid [1] violent [words], [2] slanderous [words], [3] lies, and [4] idle [talk].

|4.70| The fourfold [enemy] is: desire, anger, greed and delusion. The four enemies are to be destroyed. He, having destroyed the enemy, will become sinless.

|4.71| I shall teach you the four supports. Listen, O Brahmin. Compassion, sympathy in joy, indifference, and benevolence are the four supports.

|4.72| I shall teach you the four meditations, which will liberate you from mundane existence (saṁsāra). Meditation is taught to be fourfold: of the Self, vidyā, bhava and the subtle one[???].

|4.73| Dharma is the tattva of the Self. Vidyā in the five in a fivefold way[??]. They call [them] the thirty-six imperishable [tattvas?] ... the subtle tattva is without attributes.

|4.74| Dharma is said to be four-legged [as] it rests on the four āśramas: the householder, the chaste one, the forest-dweller and the mendicant.

|4.75| Virtuous are those who know these completely, O Brahmin. [They will experience these] the purification of all sins, and the growth of merits.

|4.76| A life-span, fame and glory and happiness grow only through dhanya. [In] a virtuous person piece, prosperity, memory/tradition? and intelligence will arise.

The eighth yama-rule: Cautiousness

|4.77| There are five areas of carelessness. I shall teach them to you, listen. Murdering a Brahmin, drinking alcohol, stealing, having sex with the guru’s wife: they call these Grievous Sins. The fifth is to be connected with them.

|4.78| A lie concerning one’s superiority, a slander that reaches the king’s ear, and false accusations against an elder are equal to killing a Brahmin.

|4.79| Defaming a Brahmin or the R̥gveda, being a false witness, murdering a friend, eating unfit or forbidden food are six [deeds that are] equal to drinking alcohol.

|4.80| Sexual intercourse with a female relative, with an unmarried girl, with women of the lowest castes, with the wife of a friend or of one’s own son are said to be equal to violating the guru’s bed.

|4.81| Stealing deposits, men, horses, silver, land, diamonds, or gems are said to be equal to stealing gold.

|4.82| If a man commits sin combining these four, by that ... CHECK These five kinds of carelessness are to be avoided, O Brahmin.

The ninth yama-rule: Charm

|4.83| [Charm has five types:] bodily, verbal and mental charm, [charm of] the eyes and [of one’s] thoughts. Giving [others] a firendly glance [is commendable] and one should avoid cuel thoughts.

|4.84| One should meditate with a tranquil mind and should speak [to other people using] gentle words. [When] a respectable person arrives at one’s own hermitage, [one should] present them with as many gifts as one can,

|4.85| with gifts of fire-wood or fire. [If] fire-wood, fire and water are easily available but are not given [as gift] or [if the phrase] ‘Live [for a hundred years]!’ is not uttered when [somebody else] sneezes, what reward could there be as a result?

The tenth yama-rule: Honesty

|4.86|

The wise who see the truth praise the five types of honesty. [Honesty] in action, in livelihood, in prosperity, in gratifying others [and ...?].

|4.87| An honest person does not approve of women, wealth, bribery and property. Honesty [means] no sacrifice [performed] in vain. Honesty [means] no austerity [performed] in vain.

|4.88| Honesty [means] no donation [given] in vain. Honesty [means] no fires [kindled] in vain. The sense faculties of an honest person are firm even if he is delighted[? CHECK]. The gods always live inside the body of an honest person.

|4.89| Thus has been taught this section on the yama-rules, O great Brahmin. Humans should follow them[?] to reach happiness here and in the other world[?]. They exist by Śaṅkara’s command as the destroyers of the filth of sins. They maintain the world and function under one royal umbrella[?-ttaḥ? ].

Here ends the fourth chapter in the Vr̥ṣasārasaṁgraha called the Section on the Yama-rules.

|4.83| My emendation from °manasā dhūryaś to °mana-mādhuryaś is based on the fact that following the list of yamas in verse 3.16cd–17ab, we need some reference to mādhurya here and that it is easy to see how this corruption came about: °mano-mādhurya° would be unmetrical, thus the form °mana-mādhurya; °mana-mā° is easily corrupted to °manasā° (not to mention the fact that manasā comes up in the next verse); in addition we need five items in this line because of pañcamaḥ. As always, I correct mādhūrya to mādhurya, although it seems that the former is acceptable in this text. I did not correct mādhuryaś to mādhuryaṁ because of the corresponding pañcamaḥ.

|4.85| Understand jātavedam as jātavedasam. See Mahāsubhāṣitasaṁgraha 2558: amr̥tāyatām iti vadet pīte bhukte kṣute ca śataṁ jīva (‘When eating or drinking, one should say: "Let it turn into nectar!"; and after sneezing: "Live for a hundred years!".’)

|4.89| °malapahārī in the MSS stands either for °malāpahārī or °malaprahārī metri causa. I could have choosen to emend it to °malaprahārī because pra and similar conjunct consonants often don’t make the preceding syllable long (‘muta cum liquida’) in this text (see e.g. pravibhāga in pāda a in this verse), but decided not to because apahārin, apahāra apahāraka are used in the text very frequently. See also 8.XX, which contains a very similar expression: sakalamalapahāre dharmapañcāśad etat.
